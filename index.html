Some interesting research that I would like to note and share (not my research, but the studies that I read).

The effect of social approval on perceptions following social media message sharing applied to fake news

By Joseph B Walther, Zijian Lew, America L Edwards, Justice Quick (2022), published in Journal of Communication
https://doi.org/10.1093/joc/jqac033
Update: 2023-02-14

Abstract:
A field experiment examined social approval in the form of Twitter “Likes” on individuals’ perceptions after retweeting a fictitious news story about a politician. The study incorporated research about feedback effects on self-perception online, partisan bias, and negativity principles. Participants read or retweeted a (verifiably false) news story via social media, and researchers appended systematic increments of Likes to the retweets. A baseline hypothesis test found no effect on perceptions due to retweeting versus simply reading a news story. Results supported a predicted three-way interaction effect between positive versus negative news story, political congruence with participants’ political party identification, and the reception of 0–22 Likes on perceptions of the politician. More Likes magnified negative perceptions of politicians, from fictitious news, when news stories were negative and focused on politicians from one’s opposite political party.

Very interesting, though very surprising why the 1st hypothesis was not supported.

Individual Inferences in Web-Based Information Environments: How Cognitive Processing Fluency, Information Access, Active Search Behaviors, and Task Competency Affect Metacognitive and Task Judgments

By Andrew J. Flanagin & Zijian Lew (2022), published in Media Psychology
https://doi.org/10.1080/15213269.2022.2085116
Update: 2023-02-12

Abstract:
Online information repositories increasingly serve as memory aids in people’s lives. Access to such information stores, however, can result in false perceived equivalencies between web-based information and personal knowledge, which can in turn influence judgments of oneself, of information search tasks, and of the Internet itself. Cognitive processing fluency, access to reliable web-based information, and actively searching for information are shown in a series of experiments to be associated with judgments related to metacognition and task performance. In the context of online information repositories accessed via web search activities, people are shown to (a) overemphasize the degree to which they find the web to be a ready source of relevant information, (b) overestimate their future task performance and the ease of tasks, and © inflate their own perceived cognitive and memory abilities. Results also show that those who are least competent in task completion overestimate their relative performance, whereas the most competent underestimate theirs, and that the availability of web-based information can inflate people’s estimated performance, particularly among the more competent. Collectively, three interrelated studies add considerable new insight regarding the impacts of near-ubiquitous access to contemporary information-saturated environments.

Very interesting.

Is e-reading environmentally more sustainable than conventional reading? Evidence from a systematic literature review

By Kang, Lu & Xu (2021), published in Library & Information Science Research
https://doi.org/10.1016/j.lisr.2021.101105
Update: 2023-02-10

Abstract:
With the increasing dominance of digital reading over traditional reading, gaining an understanding of the environmental impacts of the formats has become critical. This systematic literature review synthesizes and integrates the findings of studies comparing print reading with on-screen reading. The results reveal that the environmental impacts of printed and digital media depend on the usage rates and number of readers of both types of media as well as user behaviors and other parameters, and digital reading also has its own negative environmental impacts. Finally, research gaps are identified and a research agenda is proposed, including considering environmental performance in comparison studies, empirical investigations of actual user behaviors, and environmental savings for lending and sharing materials from libraries. This study aims to clear the misconception and change the popular stereotype that “e-reading is environmentally more sustainable than conventional reading,” and to provide stakeholders with more valuable information that is necessary to make environmentally informed decisions.

About learning stats in communication

2023-02-06

After the SEM course last semester, some students began to ask me some questions about stats. After I taught them some of the basic stats, I found actually there’re lots of similar questions. And they seems hard to tell the difference between different tools. So I have been planning to write some notes about stats based on my own understanding and hopefully, these tutorials may be helpful to learn the basic stats in communication.

From my experience in learning stats, there are only three questions that need to pay attention to if the aim is only application:

  1. What’s the aim of the statistical tool;
  2. What are the criteria to make judgment (i.e., what results in the output should be looked at and what should be reported);
  3. How to get the results by software* and what indices/coefficients should be reported;

*For this question, I wanted to remember the steps and syntax when I was a beginner. But now I never try to remember the steps and syntax. I only require myself to know what the steps/syntax are used for (if it’s syntax, then I need to know how to interpret the syntax), and where I can find the steps/syntax (e.g., google or tutorial) and then relearn the steps every time I need to use it. Sometimes I think software is the least important thing.

The table below lists the basic stats method that most commonly used (note that the language I used here is not very accurate and professional, because I think professional language sometimes impede understanding):
Statistical test Aim Criteria Software
t-test Compare means (2 groups) H0: means are equal. p<0.05, the two means are different SPSS
ANOVA Compare means (3 or more groups) H0: means are equal. p<0.05, the means are different. Post-hoc to compare each pair of means. SPSS
Chi-square test Correlation between two nominal/ordinal variables H0: no relationship. p<0.05 -> related. SPSS
Correlation Correlation between two variables (zero-order, which means without controlling other variables) H0: no relationship. p<0.05 -> related. SPSS
Regression Multivariable correlation H0: coefficient = 0. p<0.05 -> coefficient is not zero -> have a relationship; the coefficient is negative or positive. SPSS
Mediation Mediation Examining the products of two coefficients. Bootstrapping method is now widely used to examine that. For bootstrapping, 95% CI does not straddle zero. (Same logic with p-value, if contain 0 -> high probability that the coefficient is zero) PROCESS/Mplus/AMOS
Moderation Moderation/interaction p-value of the interaction term SPSS/PROCESS/Mplus
CFA Confirmatory factor analysis/measurement model (usually to confirm the scale) Model fit (e.g., Chi-square/df, CFI, RMSEA, SRMR), Factor loading, CR, AVE Mplus/AMOS
SEM Measurement model + structural morel Model fit (e.g., Chi-square/df, CFI, RMSEA, SRMR), coefficients of each path Mplus/AMOS

Some notes about this table:

  • I think p-value and the idea of latent variable are the two challenges when I was learning them.
  • I found many students seems got confused about the difference between EFA and CFA, so I highlighted them in the table.

I will update some tutorials to explain t-test to moderation with SPSS and PROCESS later on. I’ll try to use very plain language to explain them. If I have time, I’ll update the Chinese versions.

Social Influence: Direct and Indirect Processes

[Don’t have much time to read this book. So I only took some points that maybe useful very quickly]

  • Social influence is thus arguably the main currency of social life.
  • Levels of social influence:
    • Level 1 influence involves direct, person=to-person influence.
    • Level 2 influence relies on the indirect manipulation of group norms and customs and social and cultural attitudes.
    • Level 3 influence is the most elusive and indirect, when influence recipients are not even aware that their thoughts and behaviors have been influenced by others.
  • The envolvement of social influence
  • Active imagery: only be adaptive when ease of imagery is possible. It will be maladaptive when such imagery is difficult.
  • Resistance
    • Sidestepping resistance: (1) minimizing importance (2) minimizing strength of threat;
    • Directly reducing resistance: e.g., a guarantee has monetary and persuasive value because it minimizes the buyer’s risk. Or addressing counterargument in persuasive messages.
    • Disrupting resistance: distractions, disruptions, misdirection, etc. ELM.
    • Turn resistance from an adversary into an ally.
  • Dynamic social influence. Simulation.
  • Communicability. The communicability of a belief is not strictly a trait of the belief itself; it is dependent on the psychological processes of the individuals who traffic in that and other memes.
    • The perception of popularity not only conveys a means to achieve social acceptance, it also conveys information. -> some ironic and troubling consequences: (1) behavioral norms may emerge and persist unsupported by beliefs or opinions consistent with those behaviors. (2) Normative behavioral strategies are serving some goal imperfectly may remain normative even if better strategies are available (e.g., the QWERTY keyboard).
  • Coercion actions. Social control motivation+The justice motive+self-presentation (assertive self-presentation and protective self-presentation)
  • Resisting social influence [!!??]
  • Stealing thunder [!!??]
  • Intergroup beliefs. Perceived social consensus.
  • Social influence on memory
    • Tradition: eyewitness testimony
    • Social comparison theory: when people feel not confident about their memory, individuals tend to rely on information implicitly or explicitly provided by others.
      • 1st step: individuals could be not confident about their memories.
      • 2nd step: identify variables that could increase/decrease individuals’ confidence about their memory
    • Experiment 1: Individuals are more likely to recall the distinct items (salience of the item). Make some items salient by: (1) salient distractor items (i.e., nontools not previously projected),(2) nonsalient distractor (i.e., tools not previously projected),(3) salient targets (i.e nontools previously projected), and (4) nonsalient targets (i.e., tools previously projected). Using definite vs indefinite articles to manipulate social influence. [Don’t quite understand how these manipulation works…]
    • Experiment 2: Decreasing confidence in memory by reducing time for elaborating information.
    • Experiment 3: Increasing group size to increase social influence.
    • Experiment 4: Normative vs informative influence (I don’t quite understand how the normative manipulation works). The influence is informational not normative.
